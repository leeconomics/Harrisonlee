## Part 1: The Layered Island

I'm in a villa in Canggu that costs me eighty-five US dollars a night. The driver who picked me up from the airport at midnight told me, over the course of an hour-long ride, that he'd recently moved his family out of the village he grew up in because rent there had doubled in three years. He drives twelve-hour days. He earns somewhere around three hundred US dollars a month if he's lucky with bookings. My villa, for one night, costs roughly what he earns in a week of fourteen-hour shifts.

This is my second time in Bali. The first time, ten years ago, I noticed none of this. I was younger and the gap between what I was paying and what people around me were earning didn't register as a system. It registered as exchange rates being kind to me. This time I cannot stop noticing it.

The dissonance isn't the point of this series. The dissonance is the entry point. What I want to work through, across these six pieces, is what to do with the seeing. I've felt the same gap in Hawaii, where mainland-owned hotels are staffed by people who can't afford to live on the same island. I've felt it in Hoi An and in Chiang Mai, in different forms. I've read about it happening fast in Tulum and slowly in Lisbon. Tourism doesn't replace what was in a place before. It layers on top. Each layer brings its own pricing, its own population, its own relationship to the place underneath. To a visitor, a place looks like one place. To a local, it increasingly looks like several.

Researchers have been describing this for decades. Richard Butler's tourism area life cycle, published in 1980, traced the predictable arc destinations move through, from exploration to development to stagnation, with carrying capacity exceeded somewhere along the way. George Doxey's earlier irritation index, from 1975, mapped the emotional shift in host communities from euphoria through apathy to antagonism. Both models predicted where parts of Bali would end up forty years before they got there. Harold Goodwin, who has worked on responsible tourism for over twenty years and drafted the 2002 Cape Town Declaration on Responsible Tourism, defines overtourism plainly. It happens, he writes, when hosts or guests feel that there are too many visitors and the quality of life or the quality of experience has deteriorated unacceptably. By that definition, parts of Bali crossed the line years ago. A 2026 study in *Societies* journal comparing Bali and Lombok went further, framing overtourism in both islands as not really a function of visitor numbers but a symptom of systemic governance failure: fragmented policy, weak enforcement, and the exclusion of local voices from planning.

It's worth being careful here, because Bali is not a single story. The Bali Sun, the island's English-language paper, pushed back firmly when Fodor's listed Bali on its "No List" of destinations to reconsider in 2025. Local leaders, including the Deputy Chairman of Bali's hotel and restaurant association, argued that "the problem faced is more about the concentration of tourism in South Bali, not overtourism as a whole." That's true. The crisis lives in Badung, Denpasar, and parts of Ubud. North and east Bali, by comparison, are quieter, often under-visited, and would in many cases benefit from more tourism, not less, if it arrived on better terms. Even within the southern belt, there are Balinese-owned guesthouses, family restaurants, and artisan workshops that have prospered alongside the layered economy and have built genuine, durable livelihoods on the back of it. The picture isn't uniformly bleak, and pretending it is would flatten people who have built good things.

What I want to do across this series is walk through four systems where the layering is most visible in Bali. Land and housing. Labour and service. Public goods and the environment. Culture and meaning. Each one is a different lens onto the same underlying pattern. Each one ends with a few questions I'm going to try and ask myself for the rest of this trip and the next one and every one after that. The listening matters as much as the seeing, and that thread runs through each of the pieces.

I am writing this from inside the layered economy. I am in the villa. I will tip the driver. I will eat at the warung and the foreign-owned café in the same week. I'm not pretending to be outside the system, and I'm not going to land at the end of this series with a clean prescription for travelling ethically. I don't believe in overnight conversions and I don't trust virtue signalling, including my own. What I believe in is incremental change through awareness, intentionality, reflection, curiosity, and conversation. Small adjustments, accumulated across enough trips and enough travellers, do shift demand, even if the shift is slow and undramatic.

The question I'm going to ask in different forms across the next five pieces is this: what am I actually participating in when I travel here, and would I know how to tell?

Next: the system underneath everything else, which is who can afford to live where.


## Part 2: Land and Housing

The villa I'm in costs me eighty-five US dollars a night. My driver, who lives an hour away in a village he can no longer afford his old neighbourhood in, earns roughly that for two days of work. The cleaner who comes to refresh the villa each morning, on local hospitality wages around three to five million Indonesian rupiah a month, would need close to a year and a half to earn what I'm spending in two weeks. The villa owner, who I have never met and never will, lives in Australia.

This is the part of Bali's layered economy that's easiest to measure, because property prices have data attached to them. Land in Bali's southern tourist belt has appreciated between ten and seventeen percent annually over recent years, with parts of the south up over fifty percent since 2019. Rental yields for foreign investors run between seven and fourteen percent. Land in tourist-adjacent regencies like Gianyar reaches around three hundred million rupiah per hundred square metres, which is roughly eighteen thousand US dollars for a small plot. A local woman quoted in The Bali Sun, a housekeeper named Ni Made Fitri Apriyani, described saving for a home in her own birthplace as a dream she could no longer realistically hold. Her income, between three and five million rupiah a month, doesn't allow for the mathematics to work, even before religious obligations and family commitments.

The mechanism behind the price rise is straightforward. Foreign capital, mostly from Australia, Europe, Russia, and increasingly from elsewhere in Asia, found Bali's combination of climate, infrastructure, low cost base, and cultural appeal too attractive to ignore. In early 2026, Malaysia overtook Australia as Bali's largest source of arrivals, and Indian arrivals jumped by roughly eighty percent on the back of new direct flights, so the buyer pool keeps widening. Indonesia's Golden Visa, expanded in 2024, and the earlier Second Home Visa, opened long-stay residency to foreigners willing to commit capital. Leasehold structures, where foreigners cannot own freehold land but can lease it for periods of twenty-five years and beyond, gave a workable legal architecture. Villa development outpaced infrastructure. Investor newsletters tracking Bali real estate report yields that simply do not exist in Western markets, and the capital flowed accordingly.

A 2025 peer-reviewed study of Canggu specifically, published in *Cogent Social Sciences* by Suyadnya and colleagues, traced this in concrete terms. The village shifted from rice fields to built-up tourism infrastructure most aggressively after 2019. Land use changed faster than community structures could adjust. Notably, the researchers observed that open protests against overtourism are rare in Bali, because Balinese protest tends to be conducted "covertly and silently" through cultural and customary mechanisms rather than visible demonstrations. The absence of placards in the streets isn't evidence that everything is fine. It's evidence that the resistance is happening somewhere a foreign visitor isn't looking.

The honest complication is that it's not only foreigners doing this. Significant Javanese and other domestic Indonesian capital is buying and developing in Bali too. The frame is less foreign-versus-local than mobile capital versus rooted people. That distinction matters because it changes who any policy response would aim at. It also matters because it's the truth, and frames that simplify the truth tend to age badly. A clear example came in early 2025, when Indonesian authorities raided and shuttered the PARQ Ubud compound, a sixty-five thousand square metre complex in Gianyar that had been built on protected rice fields designated as National Sustainable Agricultural Land. The site was reported on widely as the "Russian Village" because of its perceived clientele, but the issue wasn't really nationality. It was the collision of mobile capital, foreign and domestic, with a regulatory regime that had been arriving slowly to a problem the system itself had created. Local commentators, including in *Broadsheet Asia*, pointed out the asymmetry: enforcement of building regulations against foreign-owned developments draws international press, while comparable violations by domestic investors often pass without scrutiny.

After the September 2025 floods, the broader regulatory picture shifted fast. Governor Wayan Koster, who in January 2025 had publicly rejected the idea of a construction moratorium, signed one into effect for 2026 across six regencies: Tabanan, Jembrana, Buleleng, Bangli, Karangasem, and Klungkung. Badung, Gianyar, and Denpasar, where most of the tourism build-out actually lives, are pointedly excluded. The moratorium is a perimeter, not a retreat. In February 2026 the province passed Perda 4/2026, which formally banned the nominee schemes that had allowed foreign capital to hold Indonesian agricultural land through Indonesian intermediaries, with criminal penalties attached. An Immigration Patrol Task Force scaled up in parallel: over a hundred and sixty-five deportations and sixty-two detentions in early 2026 across Canggu, Kerobokan, Kediri, and Ubud. The PARQ raid wasn't the exception. It became the template.

Bali isn't unusual in any of this. Lisbon ran a Golden Visa programme for over a decade that channelled foreign capital into Portuguese property and sent rents in Lisbon and Porto into trajectories locals couldn't follow. The programme was substantially scaled back in 2023 after years of housing crisis and political backlash. Hawaii has been arguing about the same dynamic for half a century, and the 2023 Lahaina fires and their aftermath sharpened the conversation in painful ways. Tulum compressed the arc into about ten years, going from backpacker beach to wellness enclave to luxury investment zone in time most other places would still be at the beginning. None of these places have solved the problem. Some have made it worse trying. What they share is the underlying mechanism: capital that can move chasing yield in places that can't move, with the people rooted to those places absorbing the consequences.

What's also worth saying, because it doesn't get said often enough, is that Balinese families are not passive in this picture. Many have leveraged tourism land economics into genuine multi-generational prosperity. Family-owned guesthouses, *desa wisata* (tourism villages) like Penglipuran and Tenganan that operate community-owned tourism on their own terms, artisan workshops in Celuk, Mas, and Batubulan that sell directly to visitors, and Balinese-owned hospitality groups that compete head-to-head with foreign operators are real, widespread, and not the exception. The harm of the layered economy is unevenly distributed. So is the benefit. Neither version cancels the other.

What's harder to defend is the specific shape the boom has taken: the speed of land conversion, the concentration in the south, the weakness of enforcement, and the gap between locals with capital and locals without. Diana Djelantik, a Balinese commentator quoted in *TIME* magazine in early 2025, framed it cleanly: "Are we talking only about the number in terms of more tourists, meaning more income, income for who?" That's the right question, and it's a Balinese question, not a foreign one.

The implication is that housing is upstream of everything else in a layered tourism economy. When the people who serve a place can't afford to live in it, every other system bends around that fact. Labour gets longer commutes. Wages stay low because the people earning them have no leverage. Public services get drawn between two populations with very different relationships to them. Culture, eventually, gets reshaped by an economy that doesn't have room for the people who carry the culture forward. All of that follows from where people can and can't afford to sleep.

I am inside this system. I'm staying in a villa I'd never be able to afford in my own city. The intentionality, for me, isn't about feeling bad about that. It's about what I do next time. For my next trip, I want to actually do the work to find a Balinese-owned place. That probably means going past the first page of Booking.com and Airbnb, both of which surface foreign-owned and corporately managed properties algorithmically. It means looking through smaller local booking sites, calling guesthouses directly even if the website is poorly designed and the email response takes three days, accepting that I might pay slightly more or arrive without the polished onboarding flow I'm used to, and being willing to wait until business hours in Indonesia to make a phone call. It's a small adjustment that adds friction to my trip and shifts a small amount of money toward people who actually live here. None of this fixes the housing market. But it changes who I'm transferring value to, and across enough travellers behaving this way, demand for locally-owned accommodation grows. That's the kind of incremental change that adds up if it accumulates.

**Questions to carry from this section:**

- What does my accommodation cost, and what does that translate to in local monthly wages?
- Where do the people who serve me live, and how far do they travel to do it?
- Who actually owns the place I'm staying in?
- If I came back in ten years, who would still be here?

## Part 3: Labour and Service

My driver this morning asked, in unprompted and very direct English, whether I had children. I said no, not yet. He said he had three, and that one of them, the middle daughter, was studying tourism management at a school in Denpasar. He was proud of her in a way that didn't need elaborating. Then he laughed and said he hoped she'd choose a different field by the time she graduated, because every Balinese family already has too many people in tourism and the wages, in his words, never grow with the prices.

The exchange was about thirty seconds of an hour-long ride. I gave him a tip at the end that probably represented less than I would have spent on a coffee in the village he was driving me toward. I will not see him again. The transaction was complete in the sense that we both got what we contracted for. Whether anything else was exchanged is the question this section is about.

The structural picture in Bali, as in many tourism-dominated economies, is that locals are increasingly staff in their own economy rather than owners of it. Walk through Canggu, Seminyak, or Ubud and the pattern is visible without needing data. Foreign-owned cafés. Australian-owned dental clinics. Russian, Australian, and European-owned villas listed on Airbnb. Western-run yoga retreats and wellness centres. The local presence is overwhelmingly on the labour side of the counter. Receptionists. Cleaners. Drivers. Kitchen staff. Yoga assistants. Therapists. The people who own the businesses, the buildings, and the brands often live somewhere else, sometimes another country, sometimes another part of Indonesia. Wages stay in rupiah. Profits leave.

That's one half of the picture, and it's important. The other half is that roughly twenty percent of Balinese work directly in tourism and another fifty-five percent, by Stroma Cole's figure, supply the tourism sector indirectly through agriculture, transport, construction, crafts, and services. Tourism in Bali isn't an external imposition that happens to locals. It's a system most Balinese have organised their working lives around, often by deliberate choice, and many have built genuinely good livelihoods inside it. The tourism management course my driver's daughter is enrolled in is a real path to a real career, and a thousand such students graduate every year. The Balinese-owned villa on the next street over from mine charges similar rates to mine and the family that runs it has built durable wealth across two generations. The artisans in Celuk, Mas, and Batubulan whose silverwork, woodcarving, and stonework fill Ubud's galleries have direct relationships with the visitors who buy from them, and the WIPO project running in Bali in 2024 helped Balinese craft and wellness entrepreneurs register trademarks and reach international markets on their own terms. Some of them now ship to Switzerland and the UK directly under their own brands.

So the labour story isn't a clean morality tale of foreign owners and exploited locals. It's a structural story about which roles offer ownership and which offer wages, and how those roles are distributed.

This isn't unique to Bali. It's the standard architecture of tourism-dominated economies. In Hawaii, mainland-owned hotels are staffed by Native Hawaiians and other locals priced inland. In Tulum, foreign-owned restaurants and beach clubs are staffed by Mexican workers bussed in from outside town. In Lisbon, short-term rentals are turned over by cleaners commuting from the periphery. The same logic applies in cruise destinations from the Caribbean to the Mediterranean. What's worth noticing is that this is the design, not an accident. A tourism economy that requires high service levels at low prices needs cheap labour available locally. That's only sustainable if labour can't afford to live where it works, because if it could afford to, wages would have to rise to keep it there. The displacement of labour from tourist zones isn't a side effect. It's a structural requirement.

The warmth question is worth handling honestly rather than ducking. Balinese hospitality is real. The smiles, the patience, the genuine interest in guests, the willingness to extend a conversation past what's strictly contractual, all of these are recognisably part of Balinese culture and pre-date the tourism economy by centuries. They're also, simultaneously, an economic product. The hospitality industry in Bali markets the warmth of its people as part of what visitors are buying. Acknowledging the second doesn't cheapen the first. The transaction is just more complicated than it looks. When I tip my driver, I'm somewhere on a spectrum between a personal exchange of gratitude and a market settlement, and I genuinely cannot say with confidence which one I'm doing or what proportion of each. He probably can't either.

Harold Goodwin, in his work on overtourism, points at something related. Tourism, he writes, has often been about consuming other people's homes. There's a story he tells from a visit to a township in South Africa years ago, where he met an elderly woman trying to start a small restaurant out of her house. He'd been invited in. When they came back outside, a coach of tourists was parked at the gate, photographing the locals through the windows. The woman turned to him and said, *they think we're animals*. The point is not that tourism in Bali is that crass. The point is that the structural relationship between visitor and resident is asymmetric in ways that the friendliness of an individual exchange can mask but never fully resolve.

The cycle, in Bali specifically, closes on itself. The driver who collects you at five in the morning lives an hour away because rents in Canggu have outpaced what his salary can absorb. His commute makes his labour cheaper than it would be if he lived nearby, because his time before and after the booking isn't paid. That cheap labour subsidises my villa stay, indirectly. The volume of villa stays raises rents. The rents push the next driver further out. Nobody sat down and designed this. It is the emergent behaviour of a system in which capital is mobile and labour isn't.

What I notice, sitting with this, is how few of the choices available to me as a visitor would actually disrupt the cycle. Tipping more doesn't fix the housing market. Choosing a locally owned café over a foreign-owned one is a small redistribution that doesn't change the structural relationship. Asking the driver about his life and listening properly is good manners but isn't policy. None of this is an argument for doing none of those things. It's an argument for doing them with clear eyes about what they are and aren't accomplishing. Small choices made consistently across a lot of visitors do shift demand, slowly. No individual visitor's choices fix the system. The system was built before I arrived and will keep going after I leave.

What does change with intentionality is the relationship. I can book the same driver across a whole stay rather than using ride-hailing apps, which means he gets the full rate rather than the platform's cut. I can pay him in cash, ask his name, ask about his family, remember the answers, and book him again next time I'm here. I can spend the marginal extra fifteen minutes finding a Balinese-owned café with food I genuinely want to eat rather than reflexively walking into the foreign-owned one with the wifi password printed on the receipt. None of this is heroic. It just changes who I'm in a relationship with while I'm here, and over a trip that compounds.

**Questions to carry from this section:**

- Who is behind the counter, and who owns the building?
- Is the warmth I'm experiencing personal, cultural, economic, or some combination?
- What would the person serving me earn doing the same work for a local?
- Am I tipping, paying, or transacting, and do I know the difference here?

## Part 4: Public Goods and Environment

My villa has a swimming pool. I haven't used it in three days because I keep forgetting it's there. The water in it gets refreshed regularly, I assume, though I haven't asked and don't entirely want to know. According to the Bali Hotels Association and government statistics, the average five-star villa or hotel room consumes around four times the water of a local Balinese household. Multiply that across the seventy-seven thousand-plus hotel rooms Bali has built since the early 2000s, plus the tens of thousands of villas, plus the swimming pools and the irrigated gardens and the laundry that turns over my towels every morning whether I asked it to or not, and you start to see the scale of what tourism uses up to host visitors.

This is the part of Bali's layered economy that has the clearest single voice working on it, which is Stroma Cole. She's a researcher at the University of Westminster who has spent over a decade studying the political ecology of water in Bali, and her recent collaborative work in 2025 extended into water governance and gender-based outcomes in Indonesia more broadly. Her data, repeatedly cited in academic and journalistic work, is concrete and unambiguous. The tourism industry consumes roughly sixty-five percent of Bali's freshwater. Water tables in some parts of the island have dropped fifty metres in under ten years. Around sixty percent of the island's watersheds are now classified as critical or drying. Wealthy tourism operators drill deeper wells, sometimes to sixty metres or more, while local households often have shallower wells of forty metres or less. The deeper wells lower the local water table, which forces neighbours to drill deeper or look elsewhere. Cole calls this dynamic, in her academic writing, a political ecology of water inequity. In a presentation she was invited to give in Denpasar, she gave it a sharper title: *Is Tourism Killing Babies?* The provocation was rooted in the data. Bali, despite being one of Indonesia's more prosperous provinces, has higher infant mortality and diarrhoea rates than the national average. Poor women bear the worst of the consequences, because in Balinese households water collection and management is largely women's work, and when local supply fails it's women's time and bodies that absorb the cost.

I'm not telling this to provoke guilt. I'm telling it because it's the cleanest example I've found of how a layered economy actually works at the level of physical resources. The water in my shower is, in a meaningful and literal sense, water that someone else cannot use. The road I'm on is also their road. The waste I generate, including the mountains of plastic I am consciously trying to minimise but inevitably contribute to, ends up in a system that wasn't designed for the volume tourism produces. The hospital I would go to in an emergency, depending on which one, is either a private clinic catering largely to foreigners or a public system that's already stretched.

The waste numbers are stark. According to a 2019 study widely cited in Indonesian and international press, including *Broadsheet Asia* in early 2025, Bali generates around 1.6 million tonnes of trash annually, including 303,000 tonnes of plastic waste. Only forty-eight percent of that waste is managed responsibly through recycling or proper landfill. Around 33,000 tonnes of plastic leak into waterways and the ocean every year. Monsoon rains in early 2025 brought what local environmental activists described as the worst plastic pollution waves they'd ever seen onto Kedonganan, Kuta, and Seminyak beaches. Volunteer clean-up crews, including many run by Balinese organisations like the IDEP Foundation, were photographed clearing beaches by hand. The waste isn't all generated by tourists, of course; it's generated by everyone living and working here, including the tourism economy that has multiplied the resident population's footprint many times over.

In April 2026 the provincial government banned organic waste at the Suwung landfill in an attempt to force separation and composting at source. The ban backfired. Within weeks, sanitation crews were pulling around seven tonnes of waste per day out of Denpasar's rivers as residents disposed of organic material wherever they could. Household burning rose. The full closure of Suwung was pushed back to August 2026. Balinese commentators were articulating the lesson in real time: infrastructure reform without a working system underneath it to absorb the change tends to move the problem rather than solve it.

Healthcare in Bali deserves a few paragraphs of its own here because it's where the layering becomes most striking. Bali now offers a full ladder of health and wellness services aimed almost entirely at foreigners. At the entry level there's yoga, retreats, traditional Balinese healing repackaged for international audiences. In the middle there's biohacking, IV vitamin drips, NAD+ infusions, cold plunges, infrared saunas, ozone therapy. At the top there's regenerative medicine, stem cell IV infusions, exosome therapy, dental implant clinics catering largely to Australians, cosmetic surgery facilities, and longevity clinics that wouldn't be permitted to operate in their clients' home countries because the relevant therapies aren't approved. Most of this exists outside the public health system. It serves people who flew in. Locals depend, meanwhile, on a public health system that's been overstretched for years relative to the revenue tourism generates elsewhere on the island. Two healthcare economies on one island, with very limited flow between them. The private clinics employ Balinese staff. The profits, in many cases, go elsewhere.

The environmental story extends past water and waste. The road network in southern Bali was designed for a fraction of current vehicle volume, which is why a thirty-kilometre journey can take two hours. Reef damage from over-visitation at popular dive sites, particularly in the islands off Bali's east coast, is well documented. Sacred sites, including major temples, see foot traffic the spaces were never built to absorb.

And in September 2025, after weeks of monsoon rain, Bali experienced what local press called the worst flooding in years. At least eighteen people died. More than a hundred and twenty flood points were recorded across seven regencies. Over two hundred tonnes of waste blocked rivers in the south. The Jakarta Post and Japan Times, in their post-event reporting, both made the same argument: this wasn't really a weather story. Between 2012 and 2019 Bali had lost over five hundred hectares of forest and around six hundred and fifty hectares of farmland, much of it in the same southern catchments that flooded. Concrete had replaced absorption. When the rain came, there was nowhere for it to go.

The Bali provincial government has been catching up. A tourist tax of 150,000 rupiah, around ten US dollars, came into effect in February 2024. The funds are nominally earmarked for cultural and environmental preservation. Implementation has been uneven. The Indonesian state news agency Antara reported in early 2026 that the levy generated IDR 369 billion (~$22 million USD) in 2025, against a target of IDR 500 billion (~$32 million USD). Compliance hovered around thirty-five percent in 2025, up from thirty-two percent in 2024. To boost collection, the provincial government has begun offering accommodation providers a three percent commission on levies they help collect, with around 150 hotels participating since August 2025. There are still no legal penalties for non-payment, and only five of the thirty-seven international airlines flying into Denpasar disclose the levy to passengers in advance. These are signs of a regulator catching up to a system that outpaced it years ago.

The provincial response, which I describe in more detail in Part 2, followed quickly after September 2025. A six-regency moratorium on new construction. A formal ban on nominee land schemes. An Immigration Patrol Task Force scaled up across the south. A quality-tourism Perda that, among other measures, will require foreign visitors to demonstrate financial standing over the previous three months as part of their visa-eligibility frame. None of this fixes the underlying economy, and some of it may turn out to be more performative than load-bearing. But the direction is now visible. Bali is moving toward gatekeeping, which is what destinations late in Butler's life cycle tend to do once the earlier signals have been ignored.

The intentional version of being a visitor here, in this part of the story at least, is partly about where you go. Bali is geographically uneven in how the burden falls. Most of the water stress, the waste pressure, the traffic, and the parallel healthcare economy concentrate in Badung, Denpasar, and parts of Gianyar. North Bali, around Singaraja and Lovina, and east Bali, around Amed and Sidemen, see a fraction of the foot traffic and would in many cases benefit from more, on better terms. For my next trip, I want to spend less of it in Canggu and Ubud and more of it in places where my presence is a smaller percentage of what's happening. Paying the tourist tax even when nobody's checking is the other piece. Ten US dollars is trivial against the cost of the trip and notional against the cost of the infrastructure I'm using. Whether the money is well-spent is a separate question. The point is to act as if the question of governance is real, because it is.

The pattern is familiar from elsewhere. Hawaii has debated tourist taxes and visitor caps for decades. Amsterdam has cruise restrictions and short-term rental caps. Barcelona has experimented with tighter regulation of Airbnb and tourist density in specific neighbourhoods. Different places, similar lag, similar contested implementation. Indonesia's then-tourism minister Sandiaga Uno warned in 2024 that if Bali saw a ten percent increase in tourists, it could face the same kind of local backlash Barcelona did. International arrivals grew twenty percent that year. The backlash, when it has come, hasn't taken Barcelona's shape. Suyadnya's point about covert and silent resistance is what to look at instead.

What the principle adds up to is this: a place's infrastructure tells you who it was built for. When you can see a piece of infrastructure serving two populations unequally, you're seeing the layered economy in physical form. The water tables drop where the foreign capital concentrates. The waste piles up where it's cheapest to dispose of. The traffic compresses around where the visitors want to be. The healthcare splits along the line between what visitors can pay for and what locals can access. None of this is hidden. It's just easy not to look at, particularly when you've travelled a long way and you're tired and the villa is comfortable and the pool, which I haven't used, is lit up at night because someone has to maintain it.

For the next layer, which is harder to see than infrastructure, we go from the physical to the symbolic.

**Questions to carry from this section:**

- Where does my water come from, and where does my waste go?
- What's overcrowded right now because I'm here?
- Am I using a service locals also depend on, or a parallel one built for tourists?
- What does this place look like in low season, and would I recognise it?

## Part 5: Culture and Meaning

There's a yoga class I walked past in Ubud, taught from a wooden platform built into a rice terrace. The instructor was a tall white woman, possibly Australian, possibly American, using Sanskrit terms for poses and ending with a Balinese-sounding prayer she'd clearly memorised phonetically. The class had about twenty students, all foreign, paying around four hundred thousand rupiah each, which is roughly twenty-five US dollars. There were no Balinese in the room, including the staff, who were behind a counter at reception. I stood at the edge of the platform for maybe two minutes and then kept walking. I'm not telling this to mock the class or the instructor. I'm telling it because of how strange it felt, in a way I couldn't immediately name, to watch a foreign person performing a version of spirituality on a Balinese rice terrace for an audience of other foreign people while actual Balinese spiritual life, which is one of the most dense and continuous religious traditions on earth, was happening in temples a few hundred metres away with no audience watching at all.

This is the layer I have the most difficulty writing about, because I'm aware that I'm doing some version of the same thing. I'm a foreign person interpreting a place I don't understand deeply, packaging the interpretation for an audience of other foreigners. The least I can do is be open about that position, rather than write as if I were outside it.

The argument I want to land in this section is that the most insidious layer of a layered economy isn't economic. It's the layer of meaning. Tourism doesn't just change who lives where and what gets built. It changes what the place means, both to the people visiting it and gradually, over time, to the place itself. Authenticity becomes a marketed product. "Balinese" becomes a brand defined as much by foreign expectations as by local practice. The version of Bali that gets posted on Instagram and TikTok diverges from the version Balinese people are actually living, and over enough time the marketed version starts to influence the lived one. This isn't a Bali-specific phenomenon. Hawaii has been having this conversation, painfully, for over fifty years. The tension between *aloha spirit* as marketed and as lived is a live political question in Hawaii in ways most visitors never see. Tulum compressed a similar arc into ten years. The Mayan iconography sold to wellness tourists in Tulum has very limited connection to actual contemporary Mayan practice in the region. Bali is somewhere in the middle of that timeline, but moving fast.

Three concrete examples are worth sitting with.

The first is ceremonies as spectacle. Balinese religious life is genuinely continuous and genuinely public. Ceremonies happen in temples, on roadsides, at family compounds, throughout the day, every day. Tourists encounter them constantly, often inadvertently. The ceremonies don't stop because tourists arrive. What changes is that they're now witnessed, photographed, reposted, filtered, and absorbed into a visual economy the participants didn't agree to be part of. The Balinese aren't performing for tourists. Tourists are spectating something real. Both things complicate each other. I've found myself, repeatedly on this trip, lowering my phone when I realised I was about to take a photo of something that wasn't for me, and then picking it up again because the photo would be beautiful, and putting it down again because that wasn't a good reason. The Suyadnya et al. 2025 study of Canggu noted, importantly, that Balinese sensitivity to overtourism often doesn't show up in the form of complaint or protest, because Balinese cultural practice tends to absorb and adapt rather than confront. That's a strength, but it also means visitors easily mistake quiet for consent. The province has started codifying responses where it can. Perda 12/2024 establishes a five-kilometre drone exclusion around major sacred sites, with *pecalang* (the traditional Balinese village security officers) now authorised to confiscate drones. RF detection has been installed at Uluwatu, Tanah Lot, and Tirta Empul. Enforcement of the ceremony as something not-for-extraction is becoming a visible state activity rather than an unspoken expectation.

The second is what I'd call the wellness aesthetic as cultural overlay. Ubud's reputation as a "spiritual capital" is largely a foreign-driven narrative built up over the past two to three decades, accelerated significantly by *Eat Pray Love* in 2010 and the wave of wellness influencers since. Real Balinese spiritual practice continues underneath it. A parallel marketed spirituality has been built alongside, with increasingly limited Balinese involvement at the ownership level. The yoga retreats are mostly foreign-run. The "healers" charging foreign visitors thousands of dollars for sessions are sometimes Balinese and sometimes Western practitioners working under loosely Balinese-coded branding. Two versions of spiritual Ubud now share the same streets. The version visitors most easily encounter is the marketed one. The version that's actually load-bearing in Balinese life is harder to access and was never designed to be accessed by us.

It's worth being careful with this argument, because the alternative reading also has merit. Foreign engagement with Balinese culture has, in some cases, helped preserve traditions that might otherwise have eroded under modernisation. The international audience for traditional Balinese dance, gamelan, and crafts has kept those practices economically viable for working artists who might otherwise have left them. The WIPO trademark and intellectual property project in 2024 explicitly worked to help Balinese craft and wellness entrepreneurs protect and benefit from the value of their own traditions internationally. Cultural exchange runs both ways, sometimes well. The question isn't whether foreigners should engage. It's who's setting the terms of engagement and who's capturing the value.

The third is the language shift. In tourist zones, English signage, English menus, and English-speaking staff have become the default. This is convenient for visitors. It also gradually displaces Balinese and Bahasa Indonesia from commercial life in those zones. Languages don't tend to recover from this kind of displacement, once it tips. The same dynamic has been documented in old tourist hubs in Thailand, in parts of Mexico, in the Algarve in Portugal. Convenience compounds over decades into displacement. A small intentionality move that costs nothing: learn five to ten words of Bahasa Indonesia or Balinese before going, and use them. *Selamat pagi* (good morning), *terima kasih* (thank you), *permisi* (excuse me), *enak* (delicious), *berapa* (how much). It's not about performance. It's about not making English the only option in someone else's country.

A Balinese voice has emerged as something close to a public ombudsperson for cultural infractions: Niluh Djelantik, the commentator I quoted from *TIME* earlier. By early 2026 her role had moved past commentary. In March 2026, when a Swiss tourist was arrested for publicly insulting Balinese culture during Nyepi, the police escorted him to her private residence as part of the process of resolution. That's not a position any culture would create lightly, and that it has been created at all is a signal worth registering.

Pico Iyer, who's spent thirty-plus years writing about being a foreigner in Asia, models a posture I find useful here. He's never the protagonist of his own observations. He's always the lens, named openly, with the limitations attached.

The synthesis point, across these four systems, is this. Land, labour, public goods, culture. Together they describe how a layered place actually works. The layering isn't accidental. It's the predictable outcome of mobile capital meeting rooted communities, of high-spending visitors meeting low-wage workers, of one economy that operates in dollars meeting another that operates in rupiah, sharing the same beaches and temples and roads. You can show this pattern in housing data, in labour data, in water data, in cultural drift. Each system is a different lens onto the same thing. The question isn't whether the pattern exists. It's what to do with the seeing.

Before the final piece, one thing worth flagging. Awareness without conversation is just observation. The harder half of intentional travel is the willingness to talk to people whose lives this system shapes, not as an interviewer collecting material but as a guest. That's a thread that runs through the parts I've already written and one that carries into the close.

**Questions to carry from this section:**

- Whose version of "here" am I encountering?
- Is the "authentic" experience I'm having designed for me, or did I happen upon it?
- What would I post about this, and what would I be advertising?
- What gets lost when a place becomes good at being marketed?

Next: what to do with the seeing.


## Part 6: Travelling Eyes Open

I've spent five sections describing what's happening in Bali. This last piece is the one I've been circling since the beginning. What do you do once you can see it?

The temptation is to land somewhere clean. Boycott. Stay only in locally owned accommodation. Refuse the foreign-run café. Travel less, or differently, or only to places that haven't been "ruined yet". Some people do this. Some of them do it well. Most of the people earnestly nodding along while reading something like this won't, including the writer of it, and pretending otherwise produces virtue signalling rather than change. Justin Francis, who's spent twenty-plus years running Responsible Travel and campaigning on these issues, was honest about this trade-off years ago when he stopped offering carbon offsets through his own company. Offsets, he said, had become a magic pill. A get-out-of-jail-free card that distracted from harder behavioural changes, like flying less. Performative ethics, in tourism as elsewhere, often replace actual ethics. The checklist becomes the destination.

The more useful version is harder. Assume you're going to keep travelling. Assume you're going to keep enjoying it. Assume you're going to keep being part of the system. Then ask what it looks like to do that with the layers visible rather than out of view, and to make small, specific, accumulating changes in how you do it.

Eyes open isn't a checklist of ethical behaviours that absolves you. It's a way of seeing that makes the trade-offs visible at the moment of choice, and a willingness to sometimes accept friction in order to make a better one. You see the foreign-owned café and the local warung on the same block. You notice. Sometimes you choose the warung because the noticing tipped you. Sometimes you choose the café because the wifi is faster and you have a deadline, and you know what you're choosing. The point isn't to always choose the harder option. The point is to stop pretending the choices aren't there, and to make some of them differently because of the seeing. Even if it's a few. Even if it's not consistent.

What this looks like in practice isn't a checklist of correct behaviours. It's a small accumulation of choices, each one tied to the system it lives inside. In Part 2, I wrote about going past the first page of Booking.com to find a Balinese-owned place to stay. In Part 3, about booking the same driver across the trip rather than using ride-hailing apps. In Part 4, about choosing the parts of Bali that are under-visited rather than the ones already over-burdened, and about paying the tourist tax even when enforcement is weak. In Part 5, about learning enough of the language to not make English the default in someone else's country. None of these are heroic. Each one adds a small amount of friction in exchange for a clearer line between the value I'm extracting and the people I'm transferring it to. Over a trip those choices compound, and across enough travellers making them consistently, demand starts to shift.

I want to be careful here not to slip into the thing the rest of this series has been arguing against. Intentional travel is not an excuse, and it's not a way to feel better about an activity that, in aggregate, takes more from places than it puts back. Done badly, it's just better-informed extraction. Done well, it's incremental change through awareness, intentionality, reflection, curiosity, and conversation, repeated consistently across many trips and many travellers, with no expectation that any individual trip fixes anything. That's as much as I'd claim for it.

It's also worth being careful about who gets cast as the villain in this story, because the easiest version is to point at foreign developers, expat villa owners, mass tourists, and influencers and stop there. That's not quite right. Mobile capital is the structural force, and it has many nationalities, including Indonesian ones. Foreign developers run the gamut from extractive to genuinely community-integrated. Expats include people who have given decades of their lives to local projects and people who've shown up last week and bought a villa. Mass tourists include backpackers who keep family guesthouses afloat and cruise passengers who barely touch the local economy. Influencers include people doing thoughtful work and people running attention-extraction operations. The category of foreigner is not the right unit. What matters is what each person does, who benefits, who loses, and over what time horizon. Be careful before villainising. Try to unpick the layers and see how each actor is contributing positively or negatively, and how the same actor is often doing both at once.

Harold Goodwin, after twenty years of working in this field, recently said the consumer is probably the most likely driver of meaningful change. He half-joked that what tourism needs is a Greta Thunberg figure. The implication is interesting. It's not that individual choice fixes structural problems. It's that structural problems built on aggregated individual choices can also be unbuilt, slowly, through aggregated individual choices going the other way. That's a more humble theory of change than the "boycott or sanctify" framing usually allows for. It's also more realistic. The Balinese academic I Gde Suranaya Pandit, writing in *The Jakarta Post* in mid-2025, made the same argument from the destination side: "As travelers, we have a responsibility. Visit mindfully, respect local culture and support small vendors and eco-friendly businesses." Coming from a Balinese voice, that hits differently than coming from a Western researcher.

The framework I want to leave with, for myself and for anyone reading, is four questions distilled from the four systems I walked through. Not a checklist. Not an ethical scoresheet. Just a way of paying attention you can carry from place to place.

1. **Who lives here, and who serves here?** This is the land and labour question collapsed. It surfaces the dual economy fastest.
2. **What's this place using up to host me?** This is the public goods and environment question. Water, waste, infrastructure, healthcare. The shared ledger.
3. **Whose version of "here" am I experiencing?** This is the culture and meaning question. It surfaces the gap between marketed and lived.
4. **What would I notice if I came back in ten years?** This is the meta-question. It forces you to think about trajectory, not just snapshot.

I'm going to ask myself those four questions for the rest of this trip. Not at every moment. That would be exhausting and a bit ridiculous. But once a day, maybe, sitting on the verandah at the end of the afternoon. They're not going to fix anything. They're going to make the trip stranger and probably more interesting, and they're going to change what I notice next time I'm somewhere else doing some version of the same thing.

The world is layered. Travel makes the layers visible if you let it. Better, in my reading, to keep travelling while letting the attention slowly change what you do, than to either boycott in performance or proceed in blindness. Awareness, intentionality, reflection, curiosity, and conversation are the levers. None of them are heroic. All of them compound.

I'll come back to Bali. I'll probably stay somewhere comfortable again, but I'll spend the hour finding a Balinese-owned place rather than the algorithmic default. I'll book the same driver for the trip, ask about his daughter, remember the answer. I'll go further north and east this time. I'll learn more of the language. I'll keep being a participant in the layered economy because that's what visiting here means. But I'll know what to look at, and I'll make some choices differently because of it. That's the most I can promise, and it's also more than nothing.


## Checklist for Intentional Travel

To use as you travel. Pull these up at the end of each day, on a long flight, or whenever you want to test what the noticing has done.

**Land and Housing**

- What does my accommodation cost, and what does that translate to in local monthly wages?
- Where do the people who serve me live, and how far do they travel to do it?
- Who actually owns the place I'm staying in?
- If I came back in ten years, who would still be here?

**Labour and Service**

- Who is behind the counter, and who owns the building?
- Is the warmth I'm experiencing personal, cultural, economic, or some combination?
- What would the person serving me earn doing the same work for a local?
- Am I tipping, paying, or transacting, and do I know the difference here?

**Public Goods and Environment**

- Where does my water come from, and where does my waste go?
- What's overcrowded right now because I'm here?
- Am I using a service locals also depend on, or a parallel one built for tourists?
- What does this place look like in low season, and would I recognise it?

**Culture and Meaning**

- Whose version of "here" am I encountering?
- Is the "authentic" experience I'm having designed for me, or did I happen upon it?
- What would I post about this, and what would I be advertising?
- What gets lost when a place becomes good at being marketed?

**The Four Anchor Questions**

- Who lives here, and who serves here?
- What's this place using up to host me?
- Whose version of "here" am I experiencing?
- What would I notice if I came back in ten years?
