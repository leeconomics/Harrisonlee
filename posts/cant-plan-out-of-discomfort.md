I once spent hundreds of hours researching how to leave consulting.

Career notes I had been keeping for two years. Spreadsheets of ex-MBB people sorted by what they had done next. Lists grouped by archetype: product, VC, founder, operator, "in transition." Dozens of coffees with the ones I could reach, plus friends of friends, second-degree contacts, anyone who had survived the jump. The whole exercise rested on a single assumption: someone ahead of me had already figured this out, and my job was to find the path with the biggest sample size of survivors.

I executed the plan. The exit was clean. The resignation was rehearsed, the next role was set up, the handover was tidy, the goodbyes were warm. I had done the thing correctly.

It was the wrong response to what I was actually stuck on.

## The plan was for a life I wasn't trying to live

The thing I was stuck on was not "what do I do next." I had a hundred answers to that. Every one of them was a version of a move someone ahead of me had already made.

That is what hundreds of hours of research will get you. A bigger sample size of other people's risk tolerance. The bigger the sample, the safer the path looked. The safer the path looked, the further it sat from anything I actually wanted, because the sample selected for what looked survivable to the people who wrote about it afterwards. The plan was perfect. The plan was for a life I wasn't trying to live.

This is what happens when you reach for the planning brain to solve a feeling. You optimise toward a destination the diagnosis never confirmed.

## Overintellectualisation as risk-avoidance behaviour

Call it what it is. When the stuckness is shaped like a feeling and the response is a spreadsheet, the planning brain is the wrong tool. It is also the most respectable tool. You can build a deck for it. You can ask people about it. You can rehearse the conversation in the shower and feel like you are doing real work.

What risk is the planning brain actually managing?

Not the risk of the wrong move. The risk of feeling the thing.

The exposed archetype is the high-functioning person whose response to every life-shaped problem is a Notion doc. A reading list for a marriage. A twelve-week project plan for a grief. A roadmap for figuring out what you actually want. Spreadsheets where there should be silence.

You know the move. Sometimes you have made it. I have.

## Why the wrong tool keeps getting reached for

Why does anyone do this? Because the planning brain is the only response to discomfort that the people around you will applaud.

Sit in the feeling and people worry about you. Build a deck about the feeling and people send you book recommendations. Quit your job to do something you cannot yet articulate and people ask if you are okay. Quit your job to take an MBA and people send congratulations.

The planning brain is rewarded because it produces the right-shaped artefacts. It performs growth. It returns to the social contract that asks: tell me where you are going next.

None of which has anything to do with whether you know yet.

## When planning is the right brain

Some stuck is planning-shaped. Clarity-stuck. Resource-stuck. Information-stuck. If you do not know how a visa works, the planning brain is the right brain. If you do not know whether to take a job at twice the salary, sometimes the spreadsheet is the answer.

Most life-stuck is not that. Most life-stuck is the body knowing something the head will not admit, and the head producing a plan to keep the body from being heard.

## What I actually wanted

What I actually wanted, underneath the spreadsheets, was to live overseas. I had no clean way to get there. The honest version required tolerating low odds, non-linear moves, a lot of grinding, and the real possibility that it might not come out clean.

The planning brain could not process that. It kept reaching for the cleaner version. The structured exit, the next role with the name on it, the path with a bigger sample size of people who had survived it.

In the end I woke up one morning and quit. No plan, no next role, just the knowledge that the plan was the avoidance. Two months later I was in Tokyo.

Since then I have felt more confident, more energetic, more grateful, more like a person living inside his own life instead of one he had talked himself into. The plan would have given me none of that, even on its best day.

You can't plan your way out of discomfort. Only through it. The plan is the avoidance dressed up as the work.
