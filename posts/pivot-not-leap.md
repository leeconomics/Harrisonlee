Two people are sitting in the same trap.

The first one has a Notion doc called "career options" that goes back three years. They have had eleven coffee chats with founders this quarter. They have a tab open right now with a company they think is interesting. They are sitting in the chair they were sitting in three years ago. They will be there in another three.

The second one quit on a Tuesday in a hot moment after a bad performance review and told everyone they were going to start something. Twelve months later they are back at a different firm, two grades below where they would have been, and the people who knew them watch them try to explain it as a learning experience.

Both of these people fell into the same trap. They sit at opposite ends of how it fails you. One stayed by overthinking. The other left by under-preparing. The nonlinear-careers genre has a lot to say about the people who get out cleanly. It has almost nothing to say about why the planner and the jumper failed in the same direction, which is that neither of them did the work that makes leaving survivable.

The discourse on nonlinear careers is romance. It tells you what the outcome looks like. It rarely tells you what the preconditions are. The people who write about it are mostly the ones who got out, and they tell their stories as if the leaving was the brave part. The leaving was the visible part. The preconditions were the brave part, and nobody is documenting them, because nobody finds preconditions sexy.

This memo is the preconditions.

## The shape of the trap

The high-achiever's trap has a clean shape. You are good at something. The thing you are good at pays well, gives you respect from people whose respect you wanted, and structures your week. The longer you stay in it, the higher the salary climbs and the more your identity fuses with the work. By year three the salary, the identity, and the social weight have all become the same thing, and they are pulling against each other so hard that you cannot tell which one is keeping you in your seat. So you stay.

The trap is not painful. The trap is comfortable. That is the part nobody warns you about. The reason most high-achievers cannot leave is not that they are scared. It is that the discomfort of leaving is more visible than the slow compounding cost of staying, and the brain prefers the legible cost to the invisible one.

The mistake the nonlinear-careers genre makes is talking about the pivot as the leap. The pivot is not the leap. The pivot is six preconditions you build before you jump, and the leap is just the visible part. If you have built them, the leap is the easiest move of your career. If you have not, the leap will mostly find a way to send you back.

I built them mostly by accident. I named them mostly in retrospect. I think they are real, and I think the absence of them is what makes the planner and the jumper fail in the same direction.

## DEPART at a glance

The acronym is DEPART. Six preconditions. None of them are the leap. All of them are what makes the leap survive.

- **D. Decouple.** Move your identity off the job.
- **E. Experiments.** Run small tests before the big move.
- **P. Permission.** Earn the right to leave.
- **A. Allies.** Expand the circle outside the lane.
- **R. Runway.** Build the savings that make refusal possible.
- **T. Threshold.** Raise the floor on what you can live on.

Read it as a memory aid, not a sequence. The operational order is roughly T then R then A then D then E then P. The acronym is the way you remember the set. The body is the way you build it.

## T. Threshold

Threshold is the floor. The number you could actually live on with no income, in a city you would actually want to be in, in a life that you would actually want to live. Not your fantasy minimum. Your real one. Most high-achievers have never run this number because the whole structure of their life has been to push the floor up, not to know where it sits.

This matters because the trap closes hardest on people who have a fantasy floor. They think they could live on a fraction of what they earn if they had to. They have actually never tried. The first six months of any pivot are usually the smaller-income months, and people who have not raised their threshold treat the smaller income as a crisis. People who have raised it treat it as the budget they expected.

The work is to find out what the real floor is and then stop fighting it. That sometimes means downgrading something visible. The apartment, the gym membership, the dinner habits. None of these are deprivations once you do them on purpose.

## R. Runway

Runway is months of cash. Twelve months is the minimum, eighteen is comfortable, twenty-four buys you the right to refuse work. The number is more important than people think. It is not just survival. It is the size of the no you can afford to say. People with three months of runway take the wrong next job. People with eighteen months do not.

Runway compounds with threshold. Twelve months of runway at the high-achiever floor might be six months of real life. Twelve months at a raised threshold can be eighteen. The same dollar buys you longer when the floor is lower.

The savings number is not heroic and it is not a flex. It is the cost of keeping your judgment intact when people start offering you jobs at month four.

## A. Allies

Allies is the conversations outside the lane. Most high-achievers have a hundred contacts who do the same thing as them and three who do not. The pivot needs the inverse.

The way you build it is by spending real time around people who are not in your industry. Not networking. Real time. Someone who is teaching law to people outside law school. Someone who has been building neighbourhood projects for six years. Someone who left banking to open a hostel in Vietnam. People in cafes who seem happier than the version of you that earns ten times what they do. These conversations do something the in-lane ones cannot do. They show you that the rules you have been internalising are local rules, not universal ones.

I started doing this without realising I was doing it. The people who shaped what I wanted to do next were almost never people in the job I was leaving.

## D. Decouple

Decouple is moving your identity off the work. This is the hardest one and the one most people skip, because the work has been giving them an identity for free and you have to choose to give that up before you have something to replace it with.

The signal that you have not done it is in the introductions. The version of me at year one of BCG led with the firm in every introduction. By year three I had stopped naming it. By the time I left, I had not mentioned consulting in introductions for so long that some friends I had made in Tokyo did not know I had ever done it. That was not strategy. That was the identity sliding off the work slowly enough that I did not notice.

The other signal is in the clothes. I went from the suit-and-clean-shoes corporate uniform to a wardrobe that was looser and more streetwear. Nobody told me to do this. The body started refusing the costume of the role before the role was leaving.

> The work has been giving you an identity for free, and you have to choose to give it up before you have something to replace it with.

The third signal is the reactions you get when you tell people you are leaving. The ones I got were almost always "good for you, but I could not do that." Or "that sounds risky, hope it works out." Or "I wish I could do what you are doing." The wish was real. The reason they could not do it was the reason this section exists. They had not decoupled. The thought of leaving felt like the thought of disappearing.

## E. Experiments

Experiments are the small tests you run before the big move. Side projects. Writing. Conversations. A weekend trial of a thing you might do for a living. The point is not that any one of them works. The point is that you have evidence about what it feels like to do the work, before you have committed your salary to it.

People who skip experiments do not know what they want. They just know they do not want the current thing, which is enough to make them leave and not enough to make them land. The planner has read about every possible next career. The jumper has tried zero of them. The way out is to have tried two or three small versions, just enough to know which one survives contact with reality.

The experiment can be very small. The year and a half off was itself a long experiment for me, but most of what mattered inside it was smaller. The writing I did. The conversations I would not have made time for inside the job. The week in a country I had been curious about. The trial of a working rhythm without the firm's calendar on top of it. Each of those returned information that I would not have got from another spreadsheet.

## P. Permission

Permission is the last one because it is downstream of the others. It is the version of leaving where you are not running from something. You are walking toward something, with the floor and the runway and the people and the identity and the evidence in place.

The signal you have permission is that you are not in a rush. The planner does not leave because they are scared of the leap. The jumper leaves because they cannot stand another month. Neither of those is permission. Permission looks like a quiet decision made on a normal weekday by someone who has already done most of the work and is just naming it.

I did not give myself permission until I was a year and a half out of BCG and travelling. There was a stretch in New York where I watched rooms full of smart people doing whatever they wanted to do, for whatever reason they had, and figuring out the rest as they went. Some of them had less money than I did. Some of them were objectively less qualified to do the thing they were doing. They were doing it anyway. An ex-banker had opened a hostel in Vietnam. Someone else was teaching law outside the law school. People I would have called less ambitious than me in my old framing seemed steadier, happier, more interested in their own lives. Permission stopped being something I was waiting for. It started being something I had been holding back from myself.

The other piece was lower. I spent some of that year in cheap and slightly grim hostels and parts of countries I had not been to. The conditions of life that most people live in, even in fast-developing countries, are simpler than the conditions a Sydney-or-London high-achiever spends their twenties optimising for. Food. Shelter. Some fresh air. Some community. Everything past that is a preference, and most of the preferences I had been treating as needs were just expensive habits I had been told to acquire. Once I had felt that in my body, the question of whether I could afford to leave stopped being interesting. The real question was whether I could afford to keep paying for the habits.

> Most of the things you treat as needs are expensive habits you were told to acquire.

There is a story I think about. I once paid two hundred dollars for a steak. People I told about it said the price was stupid. It was the best steak I had ever eaten, and they had never had one, and they were the ones telling me what it was worth. The pattern that runs underneath that story is the pattern that runs underneath staying in the trap. People who have locked themselves into a quality of life they cannot actually enjoy will tell you what your life should cost. They are not lying. They are talking about themselves.

## What this isn't

This is not "follow your passion." It is not a license to leave on Tuesday. It is the work you do before you have earned the right to call the move. The planner reads this and starts planning more. The jumper reads it and jumps. Neither is the move. The move is the boring, multi-year compounding of the six preconditions to the point where the leap is no longer the dramatic part.

## For people still inside

The window in most ambitious careers is two to three years. Year one, you are absorbing. Year two, you are compounding. Year three, the curve flattens. The right time to start building DEPART is year two, while the work is still teaching you, so that by the end of year three the leap is the easiest decision of your career.

The order matters. Threshold first, because everything else gets cheaper once you know the floor. Runway second, because it sets the size of your no. Allies third, because the new lane lives in conversations. Decouple fourth, because the identity has to come off before the leap can happen. Experiments fifth, because they tell you what to point at. Permission last, because it is the only one you cannot manufacture. It arrives when the other five are in place.

The signal that you are ready is not excitement. It is quiet. The leap stops feeling brave. It starts feeling like the next obvious move.

## Closing

People who pivot well look brave on the way out and lucky in retrospect. They are neither. They built the preconditions. They named the move after they had earned it. The visible leap was the smallest part of the work.

The trap stays closed not because leaving is hard. It stays closed because the work of making leaving easy is so unglamorous that nobody documents it. This was the documenting.
