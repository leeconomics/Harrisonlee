The common assumption is that cheaper production is good for design. Cheaper production is good for production. Those are not the same thing.

Code and design fail differently. A bad line of code is a ticket. Isolate it, fix it, ship the patch. The rest of the product sits untouched. Design sets a direction. Usability. Intent. The felt experience of a product against what a customer was expecting when they opened it. That direction has to be right before you build, not after. Move too far in the wrong direction and the cost is not a tweak. It is a rethink.

AI accelerates the build. It does not pause to ask whether you are building the right thing. ([[letter-to-designers|For designers, the loop closes differently]] — taste at the prompt level is what saves you from this.)

### Design is the surface customers actually experience

Code is hidden. Design is not. Customers interact with design directly, every session, every screen. The feedback loops — social, word-of-mouth, reviews — are faster than any internal QA cycle. There is nowhere to buffer the consequences of a bad design decision between the decision and the customer.

The speed of that feedback is not an abstraction. In February 2025, Duolingo's "Duo Death" campaign generated over a billion organic views. By May, after the company announced an AI-first operating model in language that broke with what customers had built a relationship with, it had lost over 400,000 TikTok followers in weeks. The CEO acknowledged on the Q2 2025 earnings call that the controversy had dampened customer growth. Same product. Same engineering. A design and identity decision changed the brand's relationship with its customers faster than any product change could have repaired it.

### The first thing AI produces is generic

Dylan Field, co-founder and CEO of Figma, put it plainly in a recent interview:

> <em>"The first thing AI gives you is generic by definition because it's the average of everything it's seen."</em>

Generic is fine as a starting point, if the person holding the tool has a point of view strong enough to push beyond it. [[post-ai|That point of view has a name in the rubric: Taste]]. It is a liability if they do not. AI compresses the distance from brief to output. It does not supply the judgment about whether the output is right.

Field's frame for this is clay. AI output is raw material. Someone with craft and a genuine perspective has to shape it. Without that shaping, you ship the average. Faster.

### Same customers. Different read on the room.

In May 2024, Apple released a campaign for the new iPad Pro called "Crush." A hydraulic press compressed musical instruments, paint, cameras, and creative tools down to the thinness of the new device. The creative community read it as a metaphor for what technology does to human creativity. The backlash was severe enough that Apple cancelled planned TV spots and issued a public apology. Tor Myhren, Apple's VP of Marketing Communications, acknowledged the ad had <em>"missed the mark."</em>

Samsung responded within days. A musician walks through the wreckage of the crushed tools, picks up a damaged guitar, and begins to play from sheet music displayed on a Galaxy tablet. The tagline: <strong>"Creativity cannot be crushed."</strong>

Same industry. Same customer base, the creative professionals who buy tablets to work. One brand read what those customers were feeling about AI and creative tools in 2024. One didn't. The design decision, and the instinct behind it, was the entire difference.

> The gap between bad and great designers widens with AI, not narrows. AI amplifies direction. It does not correct it.

### Bad designers don't get better. They get louder.

AI assistance has raised the floor on engineering. Weak engineers produce more passable output than before. The same does not hold for design, because design is not primarily a production problem. What matters is the quality of judgment going in. That is unchanged.

A great designer with AI tools ships a sharper vision faster. A weak designer ships a confident, high-fidelity version of the wrong thing at scale. Coca-Cola's AI Christmas campaign was widely called <em>"soulless"</em> — departing from the nostalgic, human-driven ads the brand had built its holiday identity around. Valentino's AI-generated campaign for its DeVain line was panned as <em>"cheap"</em> and <em>"tacky"</em>, the precise opposite of what a heritage luxury brand's audience expects. Neither was a production failure. Both were direction failures, built quickly.

Jaguar's 2024 rebrand — abstract visuals, no car in the launch video, heritage stripped out — resulted in 49 vehicles registered across Europe in April 2025. That is not a bug. That is the cost of a first principle set wrong and built on fast.

### The implication for how you invest

When building was the bottleneck, the logic was: ship something working first, design it better later. When building is cheap, that logic inverts. **Moving fast in the wrong design direction is the most expensive bet available.** The speed AI provides accelerates the consequence of a bad first principle, not just the velocity of a good one.

Lowering the cost of production is not the same as lowering the cost of a bad decision. For code, those two things have moved together. For design, they have not.

That is the part worth understanding before the next build starts.
