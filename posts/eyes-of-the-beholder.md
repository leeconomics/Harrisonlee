The same physical piece of cash means completely different things to different people. I keep noticing this, and two small things happened today that made me want to actually write about it.

The first was on a Grab. I didn't have card set up so I pressed cash, but the only note I had on me was a 100,000 rupiah (~$10 AUD). The ride was probably 10,000-15,000 IDR ($1AUD). When I got out I just handed him the 100k and told him to keep the change. His attitude completely changed and he said "really bro" and flashed me the biggest smile on the way out.

The second was at an ice cream bar a bit later. A young woman serving, nice but very shy. The ice cream came to 50,000 and I gave her 100k. She started counting out my change and I told her keep it. Her whole face lit up in a way that $5 could never could if an average Australian found $5 on the ground.

Not that I'm looking for those reactions or trying to have some sort of weird validation trip based on western money. But those two interactions made me realise something that sounds obvious when verablised. That is, **how relative the value of money actually is**. What may feel as little or trivial to you is humongous to another person. And it's not really about the magnitude of wealth, it's about the psychological relationship people have with money.

<div class="pull-quote">Value is not absolute. It's relative.</div>

I remember internalising this lesson when I was ~8 years old, and I dropped a 5c coin in malaysian ringgit on the floor and refused to pick it up. My parents reprimanded me aggressively in public, uncharacteristic of their usual kind and gentle demeanours. It really embarrassed and shamed me at the time, but it's moments like these that make me realise there are environments where every cent means the difference between whether you or your family has a future or not. And even once you have transitioned out of that economic position like my parents, the psychology sticks with you decades later and is pretty hard to shake.

In otherwords, that $5AUD I gave to the server is a coffee at home for me, but for her it's a story she tells when she gets home to her family, about how they don't have to worry about groceries for the week. I let my imagination loose, thinking about her going back to her auntie who she co-runs the store with and saying, "Auntie, look how much money I made today! We only had one or two customers, but one of them gave me tip that was enough for 5 customers and we don't have to worry about this week". 

People don't really think about other people's value of money when they give money away. They think about the nominal value relative to their own baseline. "Is this a lot for me?" Is this generous by my standards?" When the only baseline that actually matters is the one of the  person receiving it. And the asymmetry is already there whether you notice it or not.

What I keep coming back to is this: when you spend money in a local economy like this, you're spending less than you would at home, and what the people receiving it get out of it is mountains more. If you saw all people as equal and had even the slightest inkling to want to improve other peoples' lives, then being able to spend money in this way, and make small choices like this, can go a really long way to meaningfully changing people's quality of life.

The point of this story isn't really a "tip more! prescription. It's more about understanding the baseline people have around money. It's an encouragement to involve empathy in your relationship with money, and understand that value is not absolute, it's relative.
