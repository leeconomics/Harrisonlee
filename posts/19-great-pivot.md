The most ambitious people in consulting, law, and banking are leaving. Not at the median. At the top. The people who would have been partners in five years are walking out for startups, AI companies, and operating roles. The firms are losing the top decile of their cohort and replacing them with people who would have been second-tier hires five years ago.

The trend is not yet legible inside the firms. Promotion structures still work. Recruiting numbers still hit. The partner track still fills. The metric that matters — the quality of the people the firm is *retaining* — is the one nobody is measuring, because measuring it would be embarrassing.

### Three things flipped at once

The marginal value of optionality has reversed. Five years ago, joining BCG or Goldman or Cravath was the high-expected-value bet because the brand bought you a decade of optionality you could deploy whenever you wanted. Startups and operating roles were the high-variance side of the trade. That has flipped. Operating roles have become more legible to recruiters, more respected by peers, and more compounding for the person doing them. The brand premium has not disappeared, but it has compressed, and the discount on the alternative has compressed faster.

AI is commoditising the analytical work faster at the big three than people inside realise. The work that used to take a second-year associate three weeks now takes a partner with the right tools an afternoon. This is not yet showing up in fees, because the firms are still pricing on the old model. It is showing up in the *experience* of being a junior in those firms, where the work that was supposed to teach you something is increasingly being done by something else, and the part you do is the part that has not yet been figured out by the model.

The prestige tax has stopped pricing in. The salary discount you accepted in exchange for brand on your CV used to buy you something. A door that opened in five years. An interview at a fund. A board seat that came with the firm name. These doors still open, but they open for fewer people, and the people walking through them increasingly have operator credentials rather than firm credentials. The brand still helps. It does not buy what it used to.

### The firms haven't noticed

The exposed archetype here is the firm leadership that thinks the talent problem is a compensation problem. They keep raising salaries to retain. The people they are trying to keep are not leaving for money. They are leaving because the work feels like it is compounding more slowly than the work happening outside the firm, and no salary can fix the shape of the work.

This is the conversation the firms are not having with themselves. The retention metric they care about is partner-track attrition, which still looks fine on average. The retention metric that matters is the attrition of the top quartile, which is climbing and which they are explicitly not breaking out, because doing so would force the question of what is wrong with the work itself.

> The firms are not losing the talent war on price. They are losing it on the shape of the work.

### The harder problem is for the people inside

The conventional advice to ambitious people inside the firms — stay, build the brand, take the partner shot — was calibrated to a world where the brand still bought you something durable. That world is fading. Not gone. Fading. The new question is whether the firm is still teaching you something the market values, or whether you are paying a tax on optionality you used to need and do not anymore.

The friends I have asking this question — and there are many — share a pattern. They came in for the rigour. They learned the rigour. The rigour was real and useful. And they noticed, somewhere around year three, that the rigour was no longer the thing that was teaching them. The next two years would be the same rigour applied to the same kinds of problems for incremental compensation. Outside the firm, the same two years would be radically different, with radically more learning per unit of time.

I am one of these people. I came up through BCG. The training was real. The pace was real. The thing I noticed by the end of my time there was that I had absorbed what the firm could teach me at the rate it was capable of teaching, and the next year was going to teach me less than the year before. That was not a failure of the firm. It was a structural feature of how big firms compound a junior. The first year is steep. The second year is steep. The third year is less steep. The decision was whether to keep climbing the curve I was on, or to step onto a steeper one.

### What the firms still get right

What each of the big three trains you for is not nothing. Consulting trains structure, communication, and the ability to operate in ambiguity. Law trains rigour, written precision, and the discipline of seeing risk where others do not. Banking trains numerical fluency and a high-stakes operating tempo. Each of these is real, transferable, and one of the reasons that some of the best founders come from these backgrounds. The firms aren't dying. The top of the cohort is just leaving earlier.

The interesting question is not whether the firms are still useful as training grounds. They are. The question is how long, and for what next chapter. The shape of the answer that is emerging is two to three years, mid-twenties, then leave. Long enough to absorb the rigour. Short enough that you are out before the curve flattens and before the optionality stops compounding.

### The implication

For the firms, the implication is uncomfortable. If the top of the cohort is leaving in year three, the partnership pipeline in year ten is built from the people who stayed because they had nowhere better to go. That is not a recipe for the firm of the next decade. The fix requires changing the shape of the work, not the price of it, and changing the shape of the work is much harder than raising salaries.

For ambitious people inside, the implication is sharper. The question is no longer whether to leave. It is when, and into what. The cost of staying too long is no longer zero, the way it used to be, because the world the firm is preparing you for is being rebuilt by the people who left.

The great pivot is not just a wave of resignations. It is a re-pricing of where the most compounding work actually lives. The firms still matter. They just don't matter the way they used to. The people who have figured this out have already left, or are about to. The firms will eventually notice. By the time they do, the cohort that would have replaced the leavers will already be running the companies that the firms used to advise.
