McDonald's added a matcha latte and no one called it a sellout. Lululemon built a yoga empire and no one said they were destroying the form. Disney made Mulan, then Raya, then Moana, and the company is still treated as a serious cultural exporter. None of this is controversial. It is what global brands do.

A matcha shop in Kyoto scaling globally is selling out. K-pop is manufactured. Din Tai Fung is an ethnic chain. The Korean wave is a phenomenon. Anime is exotic.

Same act. Different verdict.

The asymmetry is not about the cultures. It is about who gets to be ordinary.

## The same act, two different verdicts

If you watch what is actually happening when a cultural form goes to global scale, the act is identical in both directions. A company takes a regional product, builds distribution, simplifies the proposition for a wider audience, prices for scale, and ships. Starbucks did it with matcha. Yoshinoya did it with gyudon. The mechanics rhyme.

The verdict does not. Western brands scaling Asian forms read as ordinary business expansion. A menu story. A category move. Asian brands scaling their own forms read as a cultural event needing framing. Anime is "exoticised" in coverage that would never frame a Disney film as exotic American culture. K-pop is "manufactured" in coverage that would never frame Backstreet Boys, NSYNC, or any of the Max Martin / Disney Channel hit factory as manufactured. Din Tai Fung is "authentic dumplings" in coverage that would never describe Starbucks as "authentic Italian-inspired espresso."

The academic name for the K-pop coding is techno-Orientalism. Julia H. Lee's 2024 essay on BTS lays it out: the discourse positions Korean artists as vehicles for the manufacture of a product, not as artists. The framing is older than K-pop. Western critics applied it to Japanese electronics in the 1980s. Toyota was, in the words of Detroit, a "mere copycat" before it became the global quality benchmark.

The pattern is the same. Asian commercial success comes packaged with a curatorial asterisk. Western commercial success does not.

## The exception that explains the rule

Toyota. Sony. Honda. Panasonic. Kaizen. None of these are exoticised today. Toyota is just a car company. Kaizen is treated as universal management doctrine, not as Japanese culture. The Walkman was a Walkman.

This is the place to be honest. The asymmetry does not apply to industrial goods. It applies specifically where the product *is* the cultural signifier. Food. Fashion. Music. Entertainment.

Koichi Iwabuchi called this "cultural odor." His 2002 book *Recentering Globalization* argued that Japanese products scaled globally only when they shed their cultural odor. Pokémon characters are stateless. A Camry is not Japaneseness, it is a Camry. The products that travel the furthest are the ones whose origin you can forget.

Cultural odor is the difference between a global brand and an ethnic specialty.

This is the version of the claim that survives every counter-example. The asymmetry kicks in exactly when consumption is identity-coded. A car is not identity-coded. A dish, a song, a film, a garment is. When the product *is* the culture, scaling it triggers the framing. When the product is mukokuseki, stateless, it scales clean.

## The infrastructure beneath the verdict

The verdict asymmetry has a real infrastructure underneath it. The reason Asian cultural commercialisation looks different from the outside is partly because, for most of the twentieth century, Asian cultural industries had less of the machinery that Western cultural industries built first. Capital. Distribution networks. Brand-building muscle. Publishing economics. The pipes through which culture travels at scale.

Three patterns show what it takes to build past that.

The first is **state-built**. Korea after the 1997 financial crisis chose to make cultural export a national project. The Hallyu Industry Support Development Plan raised the cultural budget from $14m in 1998 to $84m in 2001. The 1999 Basic Law for the Promotion of Cultural Industries set up KOCCA, the Korea Creative Content Agency. By 2023, Korean Wave–related exports hit roughly $14bn. K-pop, K-drama, Korean food, Korean cosmetics. That was a strategy with a budget, not an accident.

The second is **self-built**. Uniqlo and MUJI did not scale globally on Western retail platforms. They built their own. Uniqlo has 76 European stores and is targeting 200 in North America by 2027. K-pop's global distribution runs primarily on YouTube and tour infrastructure that Korean companies own end-to-end, not on Western intermediaries.

The third is **capture**. Japan's anime industry hit ¥2.52 trillion in 2024. Japanese studios typically receive flat production fees with no royalties, no merchandise rights, no audience data. The global distribution value flows to Crunchyroll, which Sony bought and which generated over $1bn in revenue in 2023, and to Netflix, which committed $2.5bn to anime. The cultural form stays Japanese. The economics flow elsewhere.

Here is the part that complicates the easy story. Japan tried, late, to build the first pattern and failed. METI launched Cool Japan in 2011. The Cool Japan Fund, launched in 2013, has lost roughly ¥30.9 billion. Most of its anime bets, Anime Consortium Japan and WakuWaku Japan, have been closed. Industry voices inside Japan describe Cool Japan as a joke divorced from how anime is actually made. Korea built the infrastructure deliberately. Japan did not. Anime succeeded despite that, on platforms it does not own.

This is the part of the argument that has nothing to do with judgment and everything to do with structure. The verdict is downstream of the pipes.

## The machinery no one names

Compare two boy-band machines.

SM Entertainment trainee system. Seven years of vocal, dance, language, media training. Synthetic group formation. Centralised producer-led songwriting. Choreographed releases timed to global market windows. Manufactured.

Lou Pearlman, in Orlando, in the 1990s, building Backstreet Boys and NSYNC by replicating the New Kids on the Block formula. The Max Martin and Denniz Pop hit factory in Stockholm writing much of the late-1990s and 2000s Western pop canon. The Disney Channel pipeline, from Mickey Mouse Club to Britney Spears to Justin Timberlake to Christina Aguilera. The same trainee logic. The same producer-led, synthetic, factory-built model.

Treated as ordinary industry history. Pearlman has a Wikipedia entry that reads like a normal entrepreneur profile. Max Martin is celebrated. The Disney pipeline is something parents joke about. No one calls it "manufactured" as an indictment.

The machinery is the same. The verdict is not.

This is the move the discourse has not made cleanly. Western commercial scaling of culture is treated as commerce. Asian commercial scaling of culture is treated as culture, which means it gets read through a framing layer that Western commerce skips. The framing layer is doing real work. It changes what counts as authentic, what counts as art, what counts as a legitimate global brand.

## Preservation is what happens when you can't scale

The trap on top of all of this is "preservation."

Asian cultural forms get coded as things that need to be preserved. Western cultural forms get coded as things that grow. The preservation framing sounds polite. It is also a cage. It denies Asian forms the right to evolve, mutate, simplify for scale, lose their odor on purpose. And in practice it blesses the asymmetry. If Asian culture is something to be preserved, then someone else can scale it without violating it, and the original owners scaling it themselves becomes the violation.

The exposed archetype here is the well-meaning consumer who treats Asian commercial success as a cultural artifact and Western commercial success as a company. Who reads a Japanese matcha startup going global as a loss of something pure, and a Lululemon yoga empire as a wellness category. Who is operating from a museum-studies frame they did not realise they had imported.

The work, if you care about this, is structural. Build the pipes. Pay attention to the exceptions. Korea is a study in deliberate state-backed cultural infrastructure. Uniqlo is a study in building your own retail scaffold rather than renting someone else's. Anime is a study in what happens when you keep the cultural form intact but cede the economics. These are not unicorns. They are answers.

Let Asian brands be ordinary global brands. Let the cultural odor stay on, or come off, depending on what the thing is trying to do. Stop treating preservation as virtue when it is also a side effect of capital absence.

The seat at the global table is not someone else's by right. It is whoever has built the means to sit in it.
