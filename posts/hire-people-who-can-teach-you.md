The standard model of mentoring runs in one direction. Senior people develop junior people. The assumption underneath it is that seniority and knowledge travel together. They don't.

Not consistently. Not in the places where it matters most.

### The opportunity cost argument

A CEO's job is to make better decisions than the organisation would make without them, set the conditions for good culture, manage strategic tradeoffs, and build the relationships that open doors. The role does not optimise for being the best engineer, designer, marketer, or operator in the building. Most well-run organisations need their senior leaders in the top 50 to 1 percent of competence in their domain. Very few need them in the top 0.1 percent of any specific craft.

That means by definition, in any well-built team, there are people who know more than the most senior leader about things that matter. Not as a failure. As a design feature.

If that is true, and it is, then the people who belong in the room are not only the people you can develop. They include the people who develop you. (And [[agent-that-compounds|the logic extends to agents]] — the best ones end up teaching you something back.)

### One-way mentoring is an ego structure

Hiring people you can mentor is a filter that optimises for your comfort, not the organisation's capability. It selects for people whose current ceiling sits below yours. In stable environments with slow-moving domains, that is manageable. In fast-moving ones, the ceiling gets hit quickly and the cost shows up in the decisions you make without the input you didn't know you needed.

The best hire is the one who expands the organisation's knowledge in a direction it didn't already have — [[salary-cap-hiring|usually in a direction the market hasn't priced]] yet. A hire who adds no new capability, no new perspective, no new ground-level signal is a coordination cost with a salary. That is not a recruiting philosophy. It is just the opportunity cost argument, taken seriously.

### The founder dynamic

There is a structural version of this that makes one-way mentoring not just suboptimal but unavoidable to push against.

Younger founders optimise for ambition and risk tolerance. They are native to the technologies they are building with. They are not carrying the weight of previous cycles, which is both their advantage and their gap. The people they hire to fill those gaps will often be older, more experienced, track-record-bearing. The mentoring relationship is already inverted by the structure of the problem.

Pretending it isn't is the failure mode.

This is true more broadly than founders. It is true of anyone building a team in a domain that is moving faster than any single career has moved through it. The people who know the most about what is relevant right now are often not the ones with the longest track records.

> The question is not whether you have more experience than the people you hired. It is whether you are learning from them. If you are not, the org chart is doing more work than it should.

### Hierarchy is a routing system

**Hierarchy is misread as power. It is not. Hierarchy is information architecture.** A decision-routing system that ensures the right call gets made at the right level and the right communication reaches the right people. That is its entire function.

The confusion of hierarchy with authority produces a specific and expensive failure mode. Directives go down. Context goes up, filtered and polished at each layer before it arrives. Everyone in the middle optimises for what their manager wants to hear. The actual signal degrades. By the time a real problem reaches someone with the authority to fix it, it has been translated into something manageable.

Two-way mentoring fixes part of this. Leaders who are genuinely learning from their teams receive unfiltered, ground-level signal. The learning is the channel, not just the outcome. ([[rise-of-ic-manager|The IC manager archetype is one version of this in practice]] — staying close to the texture instead of above it.)

### The exposed archetype

The exposed archetype is the manager who experiences being taught by a direct report as a loss of status. This is the person whose value is structural, not substantive. Remove the org chart and it becomes unclear what remains.

In flat organisations, or fast-moving ones, or ones where the domain is shifting faster than seniority tracks, that type is the first to become expensive. Not because they are unintelligent. Because their value was always the position, not what they brought to it.

> Design the organisation for information to flow in every direction. Not because it is philosophically tidy. Because it is faster. The team that surfaces problems, ideas, and corrections from every layer makes better decisions than the team waiting for instructions from above.

Hierarchy is the routing. What flows through it is the thing that actually matters.
