I'm writing this from Bali. The villa next door went up six months ago. It's three storeys, painted white, with palm trees out the front that didn't grow here. The palms blocked the sun off the street last week and the banana trees that used to drop fruit on the path are gone. They cut them down to widen the driveway for the next villa, which is bigger.

This isn't a complaint. It's an observation, and the observation is the point of this whole memo.

I've lived in three places long enough to feel how they work. Sydney, where I grew up. Tokyo, where I live now. Bali, where I'm visiting often enough to recognise the shape of what's happening. The trade-offs these three places have made are different from each other in almost every direction, and watching them up close has changed how I think about what a "developed" place even is.

A note before I go further. These are three cities I've spent real time in. The argument I'm going to make would look different if I'd lived in Lagos or Singapore or São Paulo or anywhere else with its own particular relationship to growth. Trade-offs are happening in every city, in every neighbourhood, in every street where someone decided to widen a driveway. I'm not writing a theory of cities. I'm writing about what watching three of them has taught me about how to stand inside one.

## What I see in Bali

The thing about Bali right now is that almost nothing is co-ordinated. Buildings go up at whatever height the owner can afford. Villas sit next to rice fields next to half-built warehouses next to dirt roads. The architecture has no shared rhythm because there's no shared body making decisions about rhythm. Footpaths exist when a developer wants them and disappear when the next one doesn't. Public infrastructure is fraying. The water table is dropping. Private compounds behind tall walls have manicured gardens and pools, and the streets outside them have potholes and no shade.

The natural texture of the place is being replaced with a performative one. Native fruit trees come down for imported palms. Walls go up where there used to be neighbourhood. Land that locals could afford five years ago can't be bought back at any price, and the locals who do still own it often sell because the next ten years of cash from a villa lease is more than they'll earn in a decade of farming. The growth is real. Money is flowing in. Foreigners are spending and locals are working. But almost none of what's being built will compound into something that belongs to the place. It's a wave of short cashflows on top of a substrate that no one is maintaining.

## What I see in Tokyo

Tokyo gave up almost everything Bali still has and got almost everything Bali doesn't. Apartments are small. Rules are dense. There's a quiet pressure to behave in certain ways, to defer to systems, to trust that someone above you is making the right call about the pension or the train timetable or the building code. Most of the time, they are. Tokyo runs because a generation of people accepted that as the deal, and another generation of planners spent the postwar decades building the scaffolding to make it true. Trains arrive on the minute. There is no trash on the street, even when the bins are full, because everyone takes it home. Walking a residential street at midnight feels safer than walking most cities at noon.

What you give up to live here is space and a certain kind of personal weight. The system absorbs your individual signal so well that the signal can stop feeling necessary. That worked for decades. It's working less well now. The younger generation is inheriting a pension system that was built for a population shape that no longer exists, and the same trust in the system that made the good decisions possible is part of why the bad ones aren't being challenged. Tokyo is the most legible city I've lived in, and the legibility is showing its age.

## What I see in Sydney

Sydney is the city I grew up in, and watching it from outside has been its own education. Sydney's trade is the inverse of Bali's. The scaffolding is strong. Every new development goes through a process that has views and submissions and council hearings and a thousand checks designed to protect the people who already live there. The coastline is preserved. The suburbs are walkable. The bush is right there at the end of half the train lines. It's a city built for a certain quality of life, and for the people who got in early enough, it has delivered that quality of life for a long time.

The cost of that scaffolding is that it was designed for a population that no longer matches the country's needs. The system that protects the existing residents also prices out the next generation. Housing costs have separated from income to a degree that almost no other developed city has managed. Immigration is being tightened to relieve the pressure on a system that should have been expanded thirty years ago and wasn't, because expanding it would have required someone to accept a trade. Sydney has the rare problem of being so good at preserving what it has that it can no longer afford to share it. The intergenerational gap is widening, and people who grew up there are leaving for cities that have less of what makes Sydney beautiful but more room to make a life.

## The pattern underneath

Three cities, three trades. The trade in Bali is texture and infrastructure for speed and short-term cash. The trade in Tokyo is individual freedom and personal scale for collective legibility. The trade in Sydney is future capacity for preserved quality. None of these trades are inherently wrong. They're answers to a question every place has to answer, and they were made by combinations of incentives and timing that almost no one alive today voted on directly.

The frame I used to bring to this, is there a right pace of development, turns out to be the wrong question. The right question is closer to: what are you willing to lose, and is there enough scaffolding in place to absorb what you're losing. Pace and scaffolding are independent variables. You can be fast with scaffolding (Tokyo, postwar). Fast without (Bali, now). Slow with (Sydney, today). Slow without is the worst combination, and most of the places I haven't lived in long enough to name are probably running some version of it.

## Why this matters as a lens

Most people, when they live somewhere long enough, end up doing one of two things with it. They romanticise it. Or they criticise it. Tokyo as anime fantasy or Tokyo as cold conformity. Bali as paradise or Bali as broken. Sydney as childhood or Sydney as the city that doesn't want you back. Both stances are spectator stances. They treat the place as something that happened *to* you, instead of as something that became what it is through a long series of trades that you can now see, if you bother to look.

The third stance is participant. It starts with asking how the place got here. What it gave up. What it got. Who benefitted, who paid, what the scaffolding does and doesn't catch. That stance doesn't always give you control. Sometimes it just gives you a clearer sense of where you stand. But it travels. The Sydney I grew up in is a set of trade-offs I didn't see until I'd lived inside someone else's. The Bali I'm in right now will look different to me in five years because of Tokyo. The lens compounds.

## What I'm not saying

I'm not saying any of these cities is the right answer. I'm not saying the trades they've made are settled. The next memo I want to write on this is about the incentives that produce these trades in the first place. Who's allowed to build, who profits, why scaffolding gets built in some places and not others. Bali isn't accidental. Tokyo isn't a miracle. Sydney didn't freeze itself by mistake. There are structures behind each, and naming them is a different memo with at least one case study attached.

For now, the observation is enough. The places we live are trade-offs we didn't get to vote on. Most of us don't notice until we've lived inside two of them. The third one is when it gets interesting.
