I'm in the back of a scooter on a road that bends to one side and then ends. The driver loops back the way we came and threads through a side lane that doesn't show up on any map. We've been moving for forty minutes. The pin on my phone, when I check it, has barely shifted. The trip is eleven kilometres and it's going to take ninety.

I've been visiting Bali often enough now to recognise the shape of this. The first few times I assumed it was an off day or a school holiday. By the fifth or sixth time I had to stop pretending it was random. There's a thing happening here that no one I've talked to about it ever quite names, and the road geometry was the moment I started seeing it.

The roads on this part of the island run north to south. They head from the mountains toward the coast, or from the coast toward the mountains, and almost nothing connects them across. If you want to get from one north-south corridor to the next, you cut through small lanes that were never designed to carry traffic, or you go all the way back to a junction near the volcanoes, or you sit on the only main road in the south of the island with three thousand other people doing the same. The geometry isn't a topography problem. The terrain would allow horizontal roads in plenty of places. The roads aren't there because no one owned the question of whether they should be.

That's the thing I want to name. Not the traffic, which everyone notices. The thing one layer down from the traffic, which most people don't.

## What's actually missing

I started looking once I had the road thing. The same shape is everywhere if you tune for it.

The water table is dropping. Wells that ran for two generations are coming up dry, and the new villas keep going in with their own bores. No one is in charge of the aquifer. Each villa pays its own water cost and the cost of the bore goes into the price of the lease. The aggregate problem is invisible at the level of any single transaction, which is why the transactions keep happening.

Footpaths exist where one developer wanted them and end where the next developer didn't. You can walk for two minutes along a graded path with a curb and a strip of grass and then find yourself stepping into a roadway with no shoulder, because the property line ended. Three-storey villas painted white sit next to dirt lanes that haven't been graded in five years. The architecture doesn't share a rhythm because nothing is asking it to.

None of these are signs of a poor place. Bali has more money flowing through it right now than at almost any point in its history. Foreign capital is buying land. Domestic operators are building scale. The villas are full. The cafes are full. Whatever metric you want to use for economic activity is up and to the right.

The thing that's missing isn't money. It's the body that would coordinate what the money is doing.

## The pool that isn't there

What I mean by that is specific. There is no shared pool of authority and budget that exists above the level of the individual property or village and below the level of the national government. Roads aren't being built across the island because no one has the authority to plan them and the budget to fund them. Aquifers aren't being managed because no actor has authority over more than their own bore. Footpaths don't connect because no one is responsible for the network. There is government in the formal sense, with offices and titles and forms. The bit of government that would build infrastructure across property lines and across communities is either absent, or weak, or busy with other things.

This is what I mean when I say a place is missing its pool. It's not a wealth problem. The money is here. It's a coordination problem, and the coordination would have to be sustained for decades to compound into anything you could call infrastructure.

## What I'm doing here

I should be honest about what this memo is. I'm a foreigner sitting in someone else's traffic. I don't live here, I'm not from here, and the experience of being stuck in a scooter for ninety minutes is a different thing from being a Balinese person whose family has worked this land for four generations. Locals know all of this. Long-term expats know it. None of what I'm naming is news to anyone who has actually lived through it.

What I can offer is the outside view, which has a use sometimes. The thing I notice that locals stop noticing because it became the background of life. The thing that became visible to me because I've lived in places where it isn't true. That doesn't make the observation more correct. It makes it more available to someone who hasn't yet sat in this traffic.

## The diagnostic

Once you have the lens, you can use it on other places. Every city you visit, every neighbourhood you move into, every place a friend mentions they're frustrated with, you can ask the same question. Is this a money problem or a coordination problem? Most of the time, in my experience, it's the second. Capital flows are visible. Coordination is invisible. We notice the absence of one and not the other, which is why we keep diagnosing the wrong thing.

Bali isn't unique in this. Sydney, where I grew up, has a different version of the same gap. So does almost every place that grew faster than its scaffolding. I wrote about the wider pattern in *The places we live are trade-offs we didn't get to vote on*. This memo is the case study underneath that one.

You don't notice good coordination. You notice the absence of it every single day, and you call it traffic, or housing, or chaos. The more accurate name for it is that no one built the pool.
